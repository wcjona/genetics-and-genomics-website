Speaker Series #2

Join us at our second speaker series event of the year to hear Mr. Sinha give a talk on his research in Cell Regeneration!

If you haven't joined Genetics and Genomics already, sign up today!

Below isa short abstract detailing the work that Mr. Sinha will present on November 5th:

Resolving tissue heterogeneity using single-cell multi-omics Single-cell sequencing has become a turning point for cell biologists as it enables profiling of single cells without experimental purifications and provides an unbiased view of cells, their regulatory states, and their interactions with neighboring cells through molecular cross-talks within intact tissues. Owing to rapid developments in technology such as droplet-based microfluidics capable of profiling thousands of single-cells by counting 3end of MRNA transcripts, organism-scale atlases have started to detangle transcriptional heterogeneity in cells comprising complex tissues. In my talk, I will share how we employed single-cell RNA and ATAC measurements to dissect the regulatory logic underlying acquisition of regenerative competence in dermal fibroblasts during skin wound healing. In-house sequencing and bioinformatic workflows developed for this project can be repurposed to interrogate cellular and molecular dynamics from complex (neural) tissues more broadly and map their response to different perturbations We can't wait to see you all on Thursday!

Speaker Series #1

Interested in Research? Join us at our first speaker series event of the year to hear Dr. Yeamen give a talk on his research in Evolutionary and Population Genetics!

If you haven't joined Genetics and Genomics already, sign up today!

Below is a short abstract detailing the work that Dr. Yeaman will present on October 15:

Same-same or totally different? Assessing variability among conifer species in the genomic basis of adaptation to climate Tree species living in temperate or boreal environments often have distributions that span an enormous range of climatic conditions, and populations from different environments are often adapted to their local climate. Transplantation of a tree from one extreme of the climatic range to another typically greatly reduces growth and potentially causes mortality. How do trees adapt to these variable environments? Does each species find some new way to cope with climatic stress, or do the same strategies get used over and over again? Answering this question can tell us more about how evolution works, and whether constraints limit the diversity of viable solutions that can be evolved to a given problem. Here, review the results from a series of projects using exome-capture sequencing to study patterns of genomic variation and association with climate, comparing patterns across lodgepole pine, interior spruce, anda number of other species. We identify 47 genes with significant signatures of convergent local adaptation, where the same gene is has evolved to adapt to climate independently in each species. will discuss how genomics can be used to answer a range of questions in evolutionary biology.
